01 Overview

Toyota's connected vehicle ecosystem had fragmented into four separate apps across different regional markets — each with its own authentication flow, visual language, and feature set. Owners with multiple Toyota vehicles had no single place to manage them.

The project brief was to design a unified mobile companion that consolidates remote lock/unlock, climate pre-conditioning, fuel and battery status, scheduled maintenance alerts, and trip history into one consistent experience — built to scale across Toyota's 40+ vehicle models sold in Latin America.

02 The Problem

Usability research with 22 Toyota owners surfaced the core tension: connected vehicle features are genuinely useful, but only when they're immediately accessible. Users reported downloading the existing apps, using them once or twice, and then abandoning them because the login friction and slow loading times made reaching a feature take longer than just doing it manually.

The deeper issue was architectural: the four existing apps had been built by different vendor teams and shared no component library or interaction language. Every new feature had to be designed four times, and bugs fixed in one app took months to propagate to the others. The product team needed a design foundation that engineering could build once and deploy everywhere.

User research synthesis — journey mapping across 22 Toyota owners in 4 markets

03 Process

Research began with a feature audit across the four existing apps, cataloguing every capability and noting where the same action required completely different flows. We then ran contextual interviews with owners in three cities, observing when and where they reached for their phones in relation to their vehicles — in parking lots, pre-departure, on the road.

The insight that reshaped the architecture: most connected vehicle interactions happen in two specific contexts — "I'm about to get in the car" and "I just parked and left the car." These aren't dashboard interactions. They're quick actions in a hurry. The interface had to be optimized for one-handed use, low cognitive load, and zero tolerance for loading states at the most critical moments.

We ran three rapid prototyping cycles, testing with 8 participants per round. The first round tested navigation architecture — specifically whether a vehicle-first or feature-first model felt more natural. Vehicle-first won decisively: users thought in terms of "my Corolla" not "remote unlock." Every subsequent decision flowed from that.

04 Solution

The Toyota Connected App opens to a vehicle card — a live snapshot of the car's current state showing lock status, battery or fuel level, climate status, and any active alerts. The four most-used actions (lock, unlock, climate on/off, locate) are available in one tap from this screen. Everything else is one level deeper.

Maintenance is surfaced proactively rather than reactively. The app uses Toyota's telemetry data to predict when a service is coming up and books a dealer appointment directly in the app, pre-populating the nearest authorized service center. Owners don't need to know what "30,000-km service" means — the app tells them what will be done and the approximate cost.
